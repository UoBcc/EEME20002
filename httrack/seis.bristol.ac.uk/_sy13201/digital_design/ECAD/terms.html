Sequential: Here ''sequential''is used in its commonly understood English form, to mean one after the other, i.e., occurring in a sequence. The term sequential is also used to refer to logic with states, as in ''sequential logic''; in comparison combinational logic is stateless. The meaning should be obvious by the context in which the word is used.

Behavioural: The term behavioural is used to describe language constructs of higher levels of abstraction. For example, Assembley language has a few native instructions such as reading and loading registers, memory access and addition. A more 'abstract' programming language such as C has much more sophisticated set of language constructs.

Assert: Assert and de-assert are terms that are used to allow you freedom of choice in choosing whether “active” is high or low. In other words, you can have an active low or active high policy, and assert would mean that signals are driven low or high respectively, to signify that they are in their active logic state. For example, the phrase "reset is asserted" can be used to signify that the reset pin is driven to '0' in an active-low reset scheme. In this scheme "reset is deasserted" would mean reset is driven high.

Combinational: Some textbooks refer to combinatorial elements. It is a matter of choice, but combinatorial can also refer to the mathematics of counting, as in combinations and permutations, and it is generally less confusing to refer to stateless logic as combinational.

VT100 terminal: A VT100 terminal is a video terminal that was manufactured by DEC in the 70's and 80's. It displayed ASCII characters, but also incorporated effects such as underlining, blinking etc.

Terminal emulator:A terminal emulator is a programme that emulates the functionality of the old text-based video terminals that were used with computers before the advent of modern monitors. The term VT-100 refers to one such video terminal made by Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC).
