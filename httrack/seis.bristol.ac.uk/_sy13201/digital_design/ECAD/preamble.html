Preamble

Introduction to Unit

This lab-based course consists of Pre-Assignment and the main Assignment. The assignment is group-based, and will be covered in a separate document. The goal of this Unit is to give you a practical introduction to digital logic design and prototyping in FPGAs based on HDL entry using industry standard tools. The Unit follows on from the introduction to VHDL in Digital Systems 20400, and is complementary to the digital design courses you have followed. It allows you to put into practice the theory and design principles you have learnt, and also reinforces many of these concepts.

It is very important that you attempt the Assignment in a structured way, without leaving yourself a mountain of work to do at the last minute. Much the best way to do this is to attend every single scheduled lab session, and bring any issues or questions you may have to the Instructors or Lecturers on the course in a timely manner.

Working with VHDL (and Verilog) for the first time can be frustrating at the beginning, as you are dealing with logic, and the cycle-accurate simulation technology is semantically quite different from a programming language. However if you follow these exercises carefully, you will gain understanding of how hardware is modelled and designed. If you need motivation, keep in mind that this is one of the most important technologies in microelectronics, and an essential foundational building block for studying any topic in Electronic System Design, not just FPGA prototyping. A lot of modern digital design consists of writing HDL code, whether it be in the development of the latest processor at Arm or Intel, designing embedded systems, developing custom ICs for any number of applications, or prototyping a design in a field programmable device such as an FPGA or CPLD. Successful completion of this course with all exercises will provide you with the fundamental understanding of the principles of HDL-based design and valuable practice implementing a significant design on an FPGA.

How to do the Exercises and Submit the Solutions Online

This document is meant to be worked through in order from beginning to end, and no section should be skipped. Questions are embedded in each section, and are of two types:

  • Tasks, where you should follow a series of instructions, such as completing a command in the simulator.
  • Exercises, where you are expected to produce some output, whether it is a worked calculation, a circuit schematic or some code of your own proposing. It is the solutions to exercises that you have to upload online.

  • Please do not go onto any part until you have completed the preceding section, as the related learning outcomes will be a prerequisite for the next section.

Avoiding Plagiarism

Plagiarism is the act of trying to pass off somebody else’s work either wholly or in part as your own and is an academic offense. Simply put, it is cheating and includes copying code, copying the answers to questions, and submitting any work that is not exclusively your own.

If you do use something that somebody else has done, give a clear reference to it. You will not get any marks for developing that module but you would not have committed an act of plagiarism. As an example, assume you are writing the code for a complex piece of hardware and want to use a binary-to-hex converter. You do not have time to develop the module yourself, and a google search turns up some code that can be used. If you use it without acknowledging the source and the fact that it is not your effort, you will receive a mark of zero, and be pulled up for a plagiarism offence. If you use the code but put in a comment in the code acknowledging the source, and clearly mention in your submission that this module is not your own effort, you will not be commuting an act of plagiarism. You will not get any marks due for developing that particular module, but you will get marks for the rest of your effort.

It is completely acceptable to reuse other people’s work, so long as you don’t claim credit for it and properly reference the source. For example, in these assignment notes, when explaining the VHDL timing model I use some figures that others have developed, or examples that have been proposed in a text book. I use these because I have checked many sources and find they are good enough to explain the concept, and there is no point doing another example from scratch. In every single case, I meticulously document the source, not just by putting a reference number next to something, but by explicitly saying that either the example or figure has been developed by somebody else, and has been adapted by me.

What if a code template is supplied, and you do some modifications, and your effort is similar to somebody else's? If you do not cheat, you will not be pulled up for a plagiarism offence. The plagiarism checking software is able to recognise and discard supplied templates, and the similarity reports will be manually scrutinised.

Tasks and exercises are numbered sequentially with a letter, A, B, C etc. They also have a header that corresponds to the parent section in the main text. For example, if a task appears in Section 2.5, and that task is the first task in the assignment, it will be numbered as Task 2.5-A. If the next i.e., second, task appears in Section 2.6, it will be named Task 2.6-B. The third task will always be named C, with the appropriate header and so on. Exercises will be similarly numbered, but have an Exercise label as in Exercise 2.5-A, Exercise 3.4-B etc., in order to easily identify and locate exercises related to a specific section. Exercises and Tasks are also displayed with a unique background colour.
